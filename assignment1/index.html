This review analyzes Gehry Getty: The Walt Disney Concert Hall Story, an interactive website that showcases Frank Gehry’s design process through sketches, videos, and dynamic interactions. With its bold visuals and seamless navigation, the site functions as a digital exhibition, offering an engaging way to explore architectural storytelling. This analysis evaluates its design, user experience, and effectiveness in conveying the creative journey behind the concert hall.

1.

What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

The first thing that caught my attention was how visually distinct and bright the website was compared to other sites. The scribbled sketches and bold colour choices immediately stood out, creating a playful and artistic atmosphere. Despite the serious nature of the project, the design choices made the experience feel more engaging and immersive. The juxtaposition of fun, bold playful font with an architectural subject was particularly striking. Additionally, the inclusion of background music was unexpected—it contrasted with the visual style, adding an extra layer of intrigue to the experience. The interactive elements, such as how different parts of the site reacted to the mouse cursor and the integration of sound, also set it apart from conventional websites, making it feel more like an interactive exhibition than a typical webpage.

5.

What is the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

The website serves as an interactive showcase for the Walt Disney Concert Hall, offering an in-depth look at Frank Gehry’s creative process. It functions as a digital exhibition, preserving and presenting the sketches, concepts, and development stages behind the concert hall’s design. Beyond celebrating Gehry’s work, the site also aims to educate visitors about architectural creativity and the meticulous planning behind iconic structure.

7.

What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time?

This website functions like a permanent digital exhibition. It’s designed for users to explore at their own pace, whether they are first-time visitors seeking an overview or architecture enthusiasts returning to analyse details more deeply. While it may not require repeated visits for casual users, those interested in architecture, design, or Gehry’s work might revisit it for inspiration or research.

8.

The website is structured as a linear, narrative-driven experience, encouraging a one-time deep dive rather than frequent revisits. Unlike a traditional blog or news site that updates regularly, this platform functions as a digital archive—users can explore the content whenever they like, but there’s no strong incentive for repeat visits unless they have a particular interest in Gehry’s work.

What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

The website closely mimics the experience of a physical exhibition:

Gallery-style presentation:

The placement of videos, sketches, and text resembles curated sections of an art or design museum.

Linear storytelling:

The website guides users from concept sketches to completed work, much like a museum exhibit’s progression.

Tactile interactions:

The site’s interactive elements—such as animations responding to mouse movement—evoke the feeling of engaging with a hands-on display.

This approach makes the experience more immersive, encouraging users to explore Gehry’s work as if they were walking through a real-world exhibition.
